The Idea Behind the Book

What if the mountain
is not the point?

"One must imagine Sisyphus happy." — Albert Camus, The Myth of Sisyphus

Camus wrote about a man condemned to push a boulder up a mountain forever, only to watch it roll back down — and he argued that we must imagine him happy. Not because the task ends. But because the task becomes his.

This is a book for children — and the people who love them — about what happens when you do something hard, every single day, for a very long time. Not because you have to. Not even because it works. But because somewhere along the way, the doing becomes who you are.

A little girl pushes a stone up a mountain. The stone rolls back down. She does it again the next morning. And the next. And the next — for years, then decades, then a whole lifetime.

The stone never stays. The mountain never changes. But a path appears. And eventually, that path feels like home.

This is a story about Stoic resilience, about finding meaning in repetition, about the difference between what we can control and what we cannot. It is also, simply, a story about a girl and a stone — and what they build together by returning to each other every single morning.

Children understand this instinctively: some things are worth doing even when they are hard. Some things are worth doing especially because they are hard.

01
Stoic Resilience

The Stoics believed we cannot control outcomes — only our response to them. The girl does not rage at the stone. She does not give up. She returns. Every morning, she chooses the mountain again.

02
Sisyphus & Meaning

Camus asked: can a life of endless, fruitless effort still be meaningful? The girl's answer — worn into the mountain as a path — suggests it can. The question is what you build along the way.

03
Repetition as Identity

By the 3,650th morning, the girl's hands know the stone. Her feet know the path. Her legs know the mountain. We become what we return to. That is how we find our way home.

04
Time & Perspective

Young and old climb the same mountain — but experience it completely differently. What looks like failure to one looks like contentment to another. The mountain does not change. We do.

From the Book

A Taste of the Story

The 112th Morning

This morning was just like the 112 mornings before it.

A little girl climbed up a very big mountain. And as she climbed up the mountain, she pushed a very big stone.

The stone was an ordinary stone, made of gray and dust and rock. The girl was an ordinary girl, who wore very unmatchy socks.

She pushed the stone past the trees, past the wind, past the mossy rocks, until she made one final shove and pushed the stone to the top of the mountain. And then it happened.

The stone tipped. First a little. Then a lot.

Down the stone went, past the mossy rocks, past the wind, past the trees. The mountain was just a mountain.

The 3,650th Morning

But the girl was taller now, stronger too. Her socks still didn't match.

She had worn a very wide path. She knew the path by heart. She knew where the moss felt best beneath her feet. She knew where the wind would cool her red cheeks.

She even knew the exact spot where the sunrise would spill gold and pink across the stream, creating a jeweled pool of water before her.

Still, the stone was heavy. Still, the mountain was steep. Still, she pushed.

Only now, her hands knew the stone. Her legs knew the mountain. Her feet knew the path.

And now there was a path. And the path was beginning to feel like home.

The 26,298th Morning

This morning was not like the 26,298 mornings before it.

The girl was now an old woman. Her paper-thin hand rested atop a walking stick. Her socks still didn't match.

Standing there in the shadow cast by the morning sun was a tiny girl, in matching socks. Together, they pushed the very big stone up the very big mountain.

At the top, the little girl looked up at the old woman. There was silence.

"Why are you smiling?" she asked. "Why aren't you mad?"

The old woman was quiet for a long moment. Then she spoke.

"Every day, I return to this mountain. Every day, I return to the stone. And every day, the path and the stone and the mountain return to me. That's how we find our way home."

"And began to walk the path back down the mountain, toward tomorrow."

Discussion Questions

For Every Reader

The Mountain Was Just a Mountain asks questions that don't have easy answers — and that's the point. These discussion guides are organized by age, but the best conversations happen when everyone climbs the mountain together.

  • The little girl pushed the stone up the mountain every single day. Do you have something you do every single day? Does it ever feel hard?
  • When the stone rolled back down, the girl glared at it. Have you ever felt frustrated when something didn't go the way you wanted? What did you do?
  • The girl's socks never matched. Do you think that matters? Why do you think she kept wearing them that way?
  • The little girl at the end had matching socks. Do you think she and the girl in the story are the same or different? How can you tell?
  • If you had to push a stone up a mountain every day, what would you think about on the way up?
  • The stone rolled back down every single time. Why do you think she kept pushing it? What do you think she was hoping for?
  • On the 113th morning, the girl stopped to listen to the frogs. Has something small and beautiful ever made a hard thing feel lighter? What was it?
  • By the 3,650th morning, the girl had worn a very wide path. What do you think that path means? Can you think of something in your life you've done so many times it's become part of you?
  • The old woman smiled when the stone rolled away. The little girl couldn't understand why. Who do you think was right — the girl who was frustrated, or the old woman who was smiling?
  • The old woman said "That's how we find our way home." What do you think home means in this story? Is it a place?
  • The book never tells us why the girl pushes the stone. Why do you think she does it? Does it matter whether there's a reason?
  • On the rainy morning, the girl slipped and almost lost the stone. But the roots she had learned over thousands of mornings saved her. How did all those ordinary days prepare her for the hard one?
  • The old woman breathed each moment in while the little girl groaned with each push. Why do you think they experienced the same mountain so differently?
  • At the very end, the little girl begins to walk back down the mountain toward tomorrow. Do you think she'll come back? What do you think her 112th morning will look like?
  • Sisyphus was a figure in Greek mythology condemned to push a boulder up a hill for eternity. The philosopher Albert Camus said we must imagine Sisyphus happy. Do you think the girl in this story is happy? What's the difference between her story and his?
  • The girl never questions the task — she just returns to it every morning. Is that wisdom or stubbornness? Can it be both?
  • The old woman says "every day, the path and the stone and the mountain return to me." What do you think she means when she says the mountain returns to her?
  • The little girl in matching socks appears without explanation. Who do you think she is? Why does it matter that her socks match?
  • The Stoic philosophers believed that we cannot control what happens to us, only how we respond. How does this story illustrate that idea? Where do you see it most clearly?
  • The girl's task is never explained and never completed. In a culture that values productivity and results, what is this story saying about the value of effort without outcome?
  • The old woman shifts from "I" to "we" in her final line. Why does that shift matter? Who is the "we"?
  • The book ends not with the old woman but with the young girl walking back down the mountain toward tomorrow. Why do you think the author made that choice?
  • The girl returns to the stone every single day for a lifetime without explanation. What in your own life do you return to every day — not because you have to, but because something in you requires it? What has that returning built in you?
  • The rainy morning is the hardest day in the book, and also the most joyful. Have you ever found that your most difficult seasons contained something unexpected — a wildness, a freedom, a joy you couldn't have found any other way?
  • The old woman feels the weight of the stone in her hands, the mountain beneath her feet, the path that has become home — before she speaks. When was the last time you felt your life in your body that way, all at once?
  • "That's how we find our way home." What is home, for you? And what is the stone you push to get there?
